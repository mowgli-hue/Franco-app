It's your first morning in Montreal. You get into a taxi from the airport, and the driver is chatting away, friendly, smiling, and you understand almost nothing. Not because your French is bad. Because French sounds nothing like it looks. The good news? There are just eight sounds that unlock most of it, and today we'll learn all of them, slowly, together. Allons-y.

Quick recap from lesson one. We learned six magic words: bonjour, merci, s'il vous plaît, oui, non, excusez-moi. Today we make them sound Québécois. We'll go one sound at a time. Repeat after me every time, out loud. Your mouth learns by doing, not by listening.

Sound one, the letter E. In English it says "ee." In French, a plain E says "uh." Listen: le, me, te, de. All "uh." Repeat after me. Le. Me. Te. De.

Sound two. Add an accent, é, and it becomes "ay," like in "say." Café. Étudiant. Médecin. That little accent is doing real work, so never ignore it. Repeat. Café. Étudiant. Médecin.

Sound three, the famous French R. Not from the tip of your tongue, from the back of your throat, a soft gentle gargle. Rouge. Merci. Bonjour. Feel it in your throat. Repeat. Rouge. Merci. Bonjour. If it feels a little rough, perfect, that roughness is the sound.

Sound four, the easy gift. The letter H is always silent. Always. You just skip it. Hôpital becomes oh-pee-tal. Homme becomes omm. Heure becomes ur. Pretend the H isn't there. Repeat. Hôpital. Homme. Heure.

Sound five. The pair O U says "oo," like moon. Bonjour. Vous. Pour. Repeat. Bonjour. Vous. Pour.

Sound six, the tricky one, because it doesn't exist in English. U by itself. Round your lips like you're about to whistle, then try to say "ee." That tension is the sound. Tu. Rue. Lune. Round those lips and repeat. Tu. Rue. Lune. If it feels strange, good, your mouth is learning a brand-new shape.

Sound seven. O N, a nasal sound. Let it buzz up into your nose, don't fully say the N. Bon. Mon. Son. And bonjour, that bon at the start. Repeat. Bon. Mon. Son. Bonjour.

Sound eight, our last one. A N, another nasal, a little more open. Again the N doesn't fully land. Dans. Grand. France. Enfant. Repeat. Dans. Grand. France. Enfant.

That's all eight. E, É, R, H, OU, U, ON, AN. Master these and you can read any French word out loud. Take a moment now and write the eight sounds in your notebook.

Let me put a few into real words. Rue. Throat R plus the round-lips U. Street. Un grand café, a large coffee, hear the nasal grand and the "ay" in café? And the big one. Bonjour. The nasal bon, the soft zh, the throat R, the "oo." Four of today's sounds in one word you say every day.
